Temporal Coherence

Quantum Temporal Coherence is the foundation of QTCL's consensus mechanism. By maintaining quantum coherence across distributed validators, we achieve Byzantine agreement without sacrificing quantum properties. The temporal dimension allows validators to reach consensus on the ordering of events across spacetime, creating an immutable audit trail.

Our ledger preserves temporal causality while maintaining post-quantum security. Each block contains coherence measurements that prove validators maintained quantum state alignment during the consensus epoch, making attacks provably detectable.

NIST Post-Quantum Standard

In July 2024, NIST standardized the first suite of post-quantum cryptographic algorithms, designating lattice-based schemes as the primary candidates for long-term security. Our implementation follows NIST FIPS 203/204/205 standards for key-encapsulation mechanisms and digital signatures.

HLwE-based cryptography provides quantum-resistant security guarantees against adversaries with quantum capabilities. No known quantum algorithm can break these primitives, making them suitable for systems designed to remain secure for decades.

Wallet Architecture

Each wallet is protected by a lattice-based key pair derived from hyperbolic learning-with-errors problems. Private keys are computed from hard lattice instances, while public keys are distributed freely. Transaction signing uses lattice-based digital signatures that remain unforgeable even against quantum adversaries.

Wallets can generate child keys via hierarchical deterministic (HD) derivation, following post-quantum variants of traditional key hierarchies. All signatures are verified against the posted public key on-chain.

Blockchain Consensus

Our blockchain uses a hybrid consensus model: lattice-based proof-of-work (PoW) and Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) agreement. Miners solve computational puzzles rooted in the hardness of hyperbolic lattice problems. Validators use threshold signatures to attest to block validity.

The Poincaré disk visualization shown above represents validator topology in hyperbolic geometry. Nodes closer to the boundary have higher latency; those near the center are validators. This topology naturally encodes network neighborhoods for efficient message propagation.

Quantum Entropy Mining

Entropy mining uses quantum-derived randomness as a source of genuine unpredictability. Each block includes entropy commitments to hyperbolic lattice noise distributions. Miners compete to find valid entropy states that advance the consensus state.

This approach decouples computational hardness from energy expenditure. Instead of hash-based work, miners produce valid random states certified by the hyperbolic lattice algebra—a more elegant and environmentally sustainable security model.
