CONSENSUS LAYER

QTCL deploys a five-node Byzantine-fault-tolerant oracle network whose consensus is certified by the Mermin M₃ inequality — a tripartite extension of Bell's theorem with classical bound 2.0 and W-state maximum ≈ 3.046. Block finality requires M₃ > 2.0, providing information-theoretic proof of genuine quantum entanglement at the consensus layer rather than classical simulation.

SPATIAL STRUCTURE

Blocks tile a {8,3} hyperbolic tessellation (Poincaré ball model, H³) — 8 octagons meeting at every vertex, curvature κ = −1. Exponential volume growth V(R) ~ e^R supports a node density that scales with network participation without topology collapse. Each spatial tile is a block; the chain is a geodesic path through hyperbolic space, not a linear sequence.

CRYPTOGRAPHIC BACKBONE

All signatures use HLWE — Hyperbolic Learning With Errors post-quantum cryptography, hardened over PSL(2,ℝ) lattices. Block entropy is seeded by a QRNG ensemble — four independent quantum sources XOR-hedged for information-theoretic security: ANU QRNG (quantum vacuum fluctuations, Australian National University), QBICK (ID Quantique Quantis hardware, Quantum Blockchains Inc.), Outshift (PaloAlto), and Random.org (atmospheric noise) — then mixed via RFC 5869 HKDF. Mining is memory-hard (4 MB SHA3-256 matrix) defeating ASICs while the tessellation depth parameter scales block reward with network-proven work.

◈ HLWE · Hyperbolic Learning With ErrorsMicro-Whitepaper
§1 · Hardness Foundation

HLWE is a post-quantum lattice scheme whose security reduces to the hardness of the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem instantiated over the isometry group PSL(2,ℝ) acting on the hyperbolic plane H². Standard LWE asks: given (A, b = As + e) over Z_q^n, recover secret vector s, where e is Gaussian noise. HLWE replaces the Euclidean lattice Z^n with a hyperbolic lattice Λ_H whose shortest-vector problem (SVP) is strictly harder due to the exponential volume growth of H² balls: |B(r)| ~ sinh(r) ≫ e^{n/2} for large r, collapsing the approximation ratio that lattice sieving algorithms exploit.

§2 · Key Generation

A keypair is a pair (pk, sk) = (A·s + e mod q, s) where A ∈ Λ_H^{n×m} is a public H²-lattice basis sampled from the QRNG ensemble, s is the secret drawn from a hyperbolic Gaussian centered at the Poincaré origin, and e is sub-Gaussian noise with variance bounded by the injectivity radius of the tessellation tile. In QTCL, oracle keypairs are delegated from wallet keys via oracle_priv = SHA3-256(wallet_priv ‖ "QTCL_ORACLE_DELEGATE_v1"), chaining on-chain accountability without exposing the root key.

§3 · Sign & Verify

Signing a message m: sample a masking vector y ~ D_Λ (discrete Gaussian over Λ_H), compute commitment c = H(A·y mod q, m) ∈ {0,1}^κ, then release z = y + c·s if z passes a rejection-sampling gate that guarantees z is statistically independent of s (Lyubashevsky's technique, adapted to H² geometry). Verification checks A·z − c·pk mod q is short in the hyperbolic metric. No random oracle strength is lost; security tightness is O(1/q) under HLWE assumption.

§4 · Quantum Resistance & NIST Alignment

Shor's algorithm provides no advantage against LWE variants — no hidden subgroup structure exists to exploit. Grover's search applies only to the pre-image search over H², which the hyperbolic SVP hardness elevates to superpolynomial even for a fault-tolerant quantum computer. HLWE targets NIST security category III (≥ 2^192 classical / 2^128 quantum). The PSL(2,ℝ) symmetry further provides algebraic noise amplification: small errors grow exponentially under the group action, making lattice-reduction attacks require exponentially many oracle queries as the hyperbolic radius increases.
